Piaget

Piaget's model outlines cognitive development. Piaget made use of schemas, which he defined to be the unique framework with which we organize our concept of the world. His model is split into four stages.

Sensorimotor Stage

The sensorimotor stage encompasses the age range of 0-2 years. Piaget defined this stage to be the phase when one's way of perceiving the world is primarily via the senses and movement.
A prominent aspect of the sensorimotor stage is the lack of object permanence, which is the ability to know that objects still exist without being able to see the object.

Preoperational Stage

The preoperational stage encompasses the age range of 2-6 years. Piaget defined this stage to be the phase where children begin to use language to express their schema.
At this stage, children gain object permanence. However, they do not understand the concept of conservation. This means that they cannot understand two things to be equivalent despite differences in appearance.
Children at this age also tend to be egocentric, meaning that they tend not to understand that other people see the world in different ways.

Concrete Operational Stage

The concrete operational stage encompasses the age range of 7-12 years. Piaget defined this stage to be the phase where children begin to use logic to make sense of events.
At this stage, children understand the concept of conservation.

Formal Operational Stage

The formal operational stage encompasses the age range of 12 years and onwards. Piaget defined this stage to be the phase where one can use logic to make sense of abstract concepts.
At this stage, people have the ability to make and test hypotheses, employ trial and error, and demonstrate metacognition, introspection, and higher order thinking.

Kohlberg

Kohlberg's model outlines moral development. The model is split into three levels, each of which is composed of two stages. There are a total of six stages.
A criticism of Kohlberg's model is that it was only designed to model males, and Kohlberg implied that female morality was less developed.

Level 1: Preconventional Morality

Preconventional morality is defined by Kohlberg to be the phase where judgements are made based on personal consequences.

Stage 1: Punishment and Obedience

In this stage, decisions are made based on whether or not one will be punished or rewarded for obeying or breaking rules.

Stage 2: Personal Reward

In this stage, decisions are made based on whether or not the consequences of an action are beneficial or not. Consequences outside of the rules are considered at this stage.

Level 2: Conventional Morality

Conventional morality is defined by Kohlberg to be the phase where judgements are made based on societal consequences.

Stage 3: Good Child

In this stage, decisions are made based on what one thinks the impact of their decision will be on their social standing or reputation.

Stage 4: Law and Order

In this stage, decisions are made based on whether or not they obey the law. People in this stage prioritize obeying authority.

Level 3: Postconventional Morality

Postconventional morality is defined by Kohlberg to be the phase where judgements are made based on abstract and personal ideas of right and wrong.

Stage 5: Social Contract

In this stage, it is recognized that laws are not perfect arbiters of morality. Decisions are made to follow the spirit, not necessarily the letter, of the law.

Stage 6: Universal Ethical Principle

In this stage, decisions are made based on personal beliefs of right and wrong, conscience, justice, and morality.

Erikson

Erikson's model outlines social development. Erickson believed that each person's personality goes through the same stages centered around a distinct conflict. Erickson's model is split into eight stages.

Trust vs. Mistrust

The conflict in this stage centers around whether or not an infant's needs are reliably met. According to Erikson, if the infant is properly cared for, they will develop basic trust. If not, mistrust and anxiety can develop.
Erikson defined basic trust to be the belief that the world is predictable and not not prone to change.

Autonomy vs. Shame

The conflict in this stage centers around whether or not a toddler can successfully execute physical skills. According to Erikson, they will either develop confidence or insecurity in their abilities during this stage.

Initiative vs. Guilt

The conflict in this stage centers around whether or not a young child can successfully plan and initiate tasks. Similar to the previous stage, they can develop self-confidence or insecurity about their independence and ability.

Industry vs. Inferiority

The conflict in this stage centers around whether or not a child can apply the abilities they developed in previous stages. According to Erikson, success will fuel feelings of confidence, while failure to apply themselves can lead to feelings of inferiority and self-doubt.

Identity vs. Role Confusion

The conflict in this stage centers around teenagers finding their sense of self and identity. According to Erikson, this is when teenagers determine who they want to be and what they want to do. If they fail to figure this out, it can lead to role confusion or an identity crisis, where people lack a sense of direction.

Intimacy vs. Isolation

The conflict in this stage centers around a young adult's ability to form relationships. According to Erikson, failure to form intimate relationships can lead to feelings of social isolation.
Erikson defines intimacy to be the ability to form close, functional relationships.

Generativity vs. Stagnation

The conflict in this stage centers around a middle-aged adult's sense of purpose in life. Most adults have something they dedicate their lives to, such as family or work. According to Erikson, not having something to work towards can lead to a feeling of uselessness and irrelevance.

Integrity vs. Despair

The conflict in this stage centers around the elderly's feelings of whether or not their life had any meaning. According to Erikson, if one feels that they did not accomplish their life goals or has abundant regrets about their past, they can develop feelings of despair and hopelessness.
